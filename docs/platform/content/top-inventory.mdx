title: Top Inventory
description: The "what's working" view in the Content section — ranks pages by views and engagement so editorial teams can see their best-performing inventory at a glance.
---

**Top Inventory** ranks the pages on the site by performance, surfacing the content that drives the most volume and the highest engagement. It is the starting point for editorial planning, redistribution decisions, and SEO maintenance.

### What the view shows

Top Inventory is a paginated table with one row per page (or page group). Each row is a clickable entry that opens a per-page drill-down with traffic source, device split, and engagement-over-time charts.

### Sort dimensions

Every column on the table is sortable; sort by any of the following to answer a different question.

| Sort dimension | Best for |
|----------------|----------|
| Pageviews | Raw volume — which pages get the most traffic |
| Unique visitors | Reach — which pages introduce the most distinct users to the site |
| Average session duration | Stickiness — which pages keep readers on the page |
| Bounce rate (ascending) | Conversion-ready inventory — pages that hold attention well |
| Pageviews per session | Discoverability — which pages successfully lead to more reading |
| Scroll depth p75 | Reading-completion — which long-form pages actually get read |

The default sort is pageviews descending — useful for a quick scan of what's getting traction this period.

### Filtering

Top Inventory respects every section-wide filter. The two most useful for inventory analysis:

- **Date range** — compare this month to last month, or this quarter to the same quarter last year.
- **Device segment** — switch between Desktop, Mobile, and Combined to see whether top inventory differs by form factor. See [`./device-segment`](./device-segment) for the segment taxonomy.

You can also filter by traffic channel to isolate, say, organic-search winners from social-driven winners — two very different editorial signals.

### Using Top Inventory for editorial planning

A few patterns the view makes obvious:

- **Evergreens** — pages that hold high pageview rank across multiple windows without a recent publish date. These deserve refresh investment because they already convert traffic to attention.
- **Spikes** — pages that vault into the top inventory for one window then disappear. Worth understanding what drove the spike (a referral, a news event) and whether the page can be turned into evergreen with editorial work.
- **Underperformers near the top** — pages that earn high pageviews but show high bounce rate or low scroll depth. The headline is doing work the content isn't. Either the headline overpromises or the content needs to be tightened to match it.

Top Inventory pairs naturally with [`./content-intent`](./content-intent), which reveals the intent mix of each page's audience — a high-volume page attracting commercial intent traffic is a very different asset from one attracting only informational intent.
