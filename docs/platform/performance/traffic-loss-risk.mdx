title: Traffic Loss Risk
description: How poor Core Web Vitals translate into SEO ranking risk — the Platform's estimate of organic traffic exposure and how to prioritize fixes by impact.
---

**Traffic Loss Risk** estimates how much organic traffic the site stands to lose because of poor Core Web Vitals. Google's page-experience signal is part of search ranking, and pages whose [p75](./p75) Web Vitals fall in the "needs improvement" or "poor" ranges are competing at a disadvantage against pages that meet the thresholds. This report quantifies the exposure and orders pages by traffic-weighted impact, so engineering effort can be spent where it produces the biggest recovery.

### How the estimate is computed

The report classifies each page by the worst of its Core Web Vitals ratings, then weights by recent organic-search pageviews.

| Risk band | Definition |
|-----------|------------|
| High risk | Any of [LCP](./lcp), [INP](./inp), or [CLS](./cls) p75 in the **poor** range |
| Medium risk | All three metrics in **good** or **needs improvement**, with at least one in **needs improvement** |
| Low risk | All three metrics in the **good** range |

The page's organic-search pageviews over the selected window are then attributed to its risk band. The result is a triage view: total organic pageviews exposed to ranking pressure, split into high, medium, and low risk.

### What the report shows

| Column | What it reports |
|--------|-----------------|
| Path | The page URL or route pattern |
| Risk band | High / Medium / Low based on the worst-of rule above |
| Organic pageviews | Pageviews attributed to the Organic Search channel in the window |
| Worst metric | Which Web Vital is in the worst rating — the one to fix first |
| p75 value | The actual p75 of the worst metric |

Sorting by organic pageviews descending within the High Risk band produces the engineering backlog. Fixing the top three pages in that list will move the site-wide signal more than fixing thirty long-tail pages with the same metric problem.

### Why traffic-weighted matters

A "poor" page with 100 monthly organic visits and a "poor" page with 100 000 monthly organic visits both contribute to the site's reputation with Google, but they don't contribute equally to traffic loss. Traffic-weighted impact is the only correct lens for prioritization because:

- Ranking changes affect each page independently — the high-volume page losing a position costs hundreds of times more visits than the low-volume page losing the same position.
- Engineering effort to fix a slow page is roughly constant regardless of the page's traffic. Effort spent on high-volume pages has dramatically better return.

### What the report deliberately does not claim

This report estimates exposure, not certainty. Google's ranking algorithm is opaque, multi-signal, and changes regularly. A page in the High Risk band is at structural disadvantage; it is not guaranteed to lose traffic, and fixing it is not guaranteed to recover any specific amount. Use the report to **order** fix work, not to **forecast** traffic.
